    The Hollow of Seven Cedars¶

    The old lovers' grove. A pool. Seven trees. A spirit that eats what it is given.

    Overview¶
    Name The Hollow of Seven Cedars (formerly Lovers' Grove)
    Type Ancient ritual site / sacred grove
    Location A few days' travel northwest of the Palatine Eye Vault; roughly west of Willowshore, across the river
    Status CORRUPTED. Kurosawa already completed the Root key here — the party arrived to find the rite done, not pending. Ginkgo and Boone tore out the master graft; a Warden of the Grove rose in answer

    Session Eleven — the key was already turned

    The party reached the Hollow expecting to prevent the Root rite. They were too late. Kurosawa performed the grafting months ago — the cedars are already bleeding, the basin already inverted, the corruption already reaching toward Willowshore. What the party did here was not prevention but triage, and the cost of stopping it appears to be the death of the grove itself.

    Description¶

    The Hollow of Seven Cedars is an ancient and rarely visited place west of Willowshore. Seven enormous cedars — redwood-thick, eons old — stand in a near-perfect ring, their high branches woven into a closed canopy. At the heart of the ring is an almost perfectly round stone basin, like an oversized bird-bath, ringed by ivory-white alabaster stones. In older days, when more people knew the road, it was called simply Lovers' Grove.

    The site is held to be the home of a dream spirit — sometimes called the Dream Eater, or the Grandmother. Tradition holds that the spirit's gender is unknown and unimportant; it accepts what is given to it and asks nothing else.

    As the party found it (Session Eleven)¶

    The grove was wrong long before the party reached the ring. On the approach trail they found a cut red ribbon and a broken child's toy — grave-tokens that belonged at the grove, somehow strewn away from it. The forest itself was healthy (no wounded-land sickness like the Dark Woods), but Ginkgo felt a dissociation in the air, and an Augury confirmed something was deeply off.

    Inside the ring:

    • The white alabaster stones were blackened and marbled, marred where they had been unblemished
    • The central pool was inky, light-swallowing black — no reflection, no shimmer
    • Heavy copper nails — railroad-tie thick, sledge-driven — were hammered into every cedar, bark-side facing the basin, wounding the trees. Black, inky sap bled from each wound, exactly like the corruption at the Mourndusk Willow
    • On the third cedar, ~20 inches off the ground, frantic knife-scratches had gouged out a carved name, leaving only the letters ·S·S·I·N·V·O·S·S — Cassian Voss. In the gouges: a fine khaki/mustard powder, identified as finely-ground moth-wing carapace — the same signature as the moths that killed the lanterns at the mayor's inauguration and the wings found at the willow

    The Grandmother / Dream Eater¶

    The grove's spirit went by several names — most often the Grandmother or the Dream Eater. The legend Littlefinger and Donkey recalled (Society 23) holds that if she answered your plea, she would eat the dark memory you brought her — the grief, the guilt, the thing you could not carry — and let you keep the good. The eaten memory was said to poison her in your place; that was the bargain. Pilgrims always brought a memento (a ribbon, a soldier's medal, a dead child's toy) as the anchor of the thing to be removed.

    The function was fundamentally subtractive and protective: she took the worst of what people remembered so the rest could heal. Kurosawa's corruption inverts exactly this — see below.

    The Old Practice¶

    Pilgrims came to the Hollow to cast off the dreams and desires they wished to be free of, and to keep the ones they cherished.

    The rite, as Mido recalls it:

    1. Bring a trinket that anchors the unwanted dream — a child's toy, a soldier's badge, a lover's token, an emblem of war
    2. Place the trinket inside one of the seven cedars (the trees have been worn smooth by hands and grief)
    3. Speak the dream or memory into the pool at the heart of the grove
    4. Walk away changed — relieved of the harshest edge of the thing remembered, but not of the memory itself

    People came here for many reasons:

    • A spouse with a wandering eye, asking for the desire to be removed
    • A grieving parent, asking to keep love for a dead child but not the daily, gnawing pain
    • Veterans of old wars, depositing badges and dog-tags they could no longer carry
    • Lovers who wished to forget another lover, or to remember only the good of a parted one

    The place has long been considered private and sacred — even those who do not believe in spirits avert their eyes when passing nearby.

    Significance to Kurosawa's Plan¶

    Kurosawa's recovered note (the Note of Five Keys) names the Hollow as the second of his five ley-line targets. He labels it the Root key:

    "The old lovers' grove, now called the Hollow of Seven Cedars, must be opened at the root next. Memory can be cut, grafted, and made to grow in a new direction."

    Mido interprets this as Kurosawa intending to invert the spirit's old function — instead of helping people cast off unwanted memory, the Dream Eater would be made to prune and graft memory in the townspeople of Willowshore. What the survivors would forget — and what they would remember instead — would be Kurosawa's choice.

    "Will the surviving people forget their loved ones? Will they remember only the dark thoughts? I'm not exactly sure what he wants with it, but it seems very important to his next phase." — Mido

    If the legends are true and the spirit still exists, it is presumably still bound to the cedars and the pool by the same agreement that held it there for centuries.

    Kurosawa's Rite (the vision in the pool)¶

    When Da Baishan touched the black water — drawn by the eye-mark warming on the back of his hand — the basin showed him, fisheye-from-below, what was done here. It was the first time the party has watched Kurosawa perform a key ritual:

    • Kurosawa knelt alone at the basin under recent moonlight, hands black with wet soil, a sledge-mallet and a pouch of long copper nails at his side
    • He held a severed, blackened root about a foot and a half long, which squirmed hungrily toward the water as he chanted
    • He spoke an incantation — the line he mumbled, that the vision carried up to the party:

    "Memory is not sacred. It is only precedent. And precedent can be overturned."

    • He pressed the root into the water; its inky coating bled off into the pool; it grafted itself onto one of the great cedar roots in the basin floor and the blackness streaked outward through all seven interconnected root systems

    The vision then cut to Willowshore to show the purpose: husbands remembering only disappointment, children remembering only fear, neighbors forgetting debts of kindness. Liwen looking at the party without recognition. Radiant Willow lowering a hand she had once raised for them — no longer able to remember why she trusted them. Yong keeping the memory of his rescued son, but fixating instead on the belief that the party caused the danger in the first place. The grove's old gift — eat the dark, keep the good — turned exactly inside out, the corruption flowing from the Seven Cedars into the town.

    This is the Root key in operation: not a weapon, but a machine for poisoning a town's memory of itself.

    The Verdict: The Trees Must Die¶

    The vision left Da Baishan with one final, flat truth, and Ginkgo's own consultation with the mycelium (Nature/ecology 21) confirmed it:

    • The poison is in all seven cedars' interconnected roots, not just the grafted one
    • Pulling the nails is not enough. Draining the basin is not enough. Tearing out the master graft is not enough
    • There is no known cure and no extraction — this is arcane/occult corruption, not a natural blight or poison that can be salved
    • The only paths even hypothesized: knowing the originating ritual (to reverse it), or going back before it was implemented
    • To stop the corruption, the grove itself — all seven ancient cedars — must die. Killing the grove also presumably ends the Dream Eater's gift forever

    Ginkgo, a leshy, found this almost impossible to accept ("these are old trees, older than all of us") — and then jumped into the pool anyway to try.

    The Pool's Second Vision (Ginkgo)¶

    When Ginkgo leapt in, the basin showed him a different scene — a test rather than a record: two childhood friends laughing on a bench; one called to war and one not; they swear they will never be enemies. Seasons blur; a battlefield; two soldiers duel; the victor lifts the slain one's helmet and it is the friend who went to war. The water asked:

    "Who do you choose? Even when there is no blood law or prophet bind — who would you save?"

    Ginkgo answered: his childhood friend from the village he left behind. The vision honored the answer, and the water drained into the earth, leaving him at the bottom of the dry basin beside the throbbing, heartbeat-alive master graft.

    The Warden of the Grove¶

    The instant Boone ripped the master graft free (Athletics 25) — black ichor splattering, the root writhing for fifteen seconds before going limp and ashen — the trees outside the ring swayed and a horned creature tore out of the treeline, howling at the sky. Da Baishan's Occultism identified it: a Warden of the Grove, the grove's protector, and it looked angry.

    The session ended on its arrival. The party's dilemma is sharp: the only known way to save Willowshore's memory is to kill the grove the Warden exists to protect. Whether the next session opens in combat or in a desperate attempt at diplomacy is unresolved.

    Approach¶

    Roughly 10 miles west of Willowshore. The trail passes through scattered woods that thicken as the road descends toward the grove. The cedars are visible from a distance — seven dark spires above the canopy, taller than the surrounding trees, set in a near-perfect ring.

    Open Questions¶

    • Will the party actually kill the grove? It is the only known cure, but it ends the Dream Eater's gift forever — and a Warden now stands in the way
    • Can the Warden be reasoned with? The party's goal (destroy the corruption) and its goal (protect the grove) point in opposite directions — unless the grove's death is the mercy
    • Is the Dream Eater / Grandmother still alive inside the corruption the way the corrupted leshies were? If she is a hostage, killing the trees may also kill her
    • What killed Cassian Voss here? His name was carved into the third cedar and then scratched out, with moth-wing powder in the gouges, six months to a year ago — months before Kurosawa's nails. He came to investigate the leyline and never left
    • Who scratched out his name, and why — and does the older, fainter magic in that carving belong to Voss or to whatever killed him?
    • Reversibility. The pool itself suggested two impossibilities — know the originating ritual or go back before it was done. Is either actually reachable?

    • The party deferred travel to the Hollow in favor of going south first, to the Order of the Palatine Eye (~50 miles away) for occult counsel before facing the Dream Eater

    • The party arrived from the southwest to find the grove already corrupted — Kurosawa's Root rite was complete, not pending

    • Da Baishan touched the pool and witnessed Kurosawa performing the grafting rite, including the incantation "Memory is not sacred. It is only precedent. And precedent can be overturned," and the projected fate of Willowshore's memory
    • The pool's final truth + Ginkgo's mycelium check confirmed the trees must die — no cure, no extraction

    • Boone tore out the master graft root (Athletics 25) — and a Warden of the Grove erupted from the treeline, howling. Session ended on its arrival.
